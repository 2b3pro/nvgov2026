Federal preemption, local discretion

Who Actually Controls Immigration?

Immigration law is federal, but the Supreme Court’s Arizona v. United States decision left space for voluntary cooperation. Nevada has no statewide sanctuary or anti-sanctuary statute, so each sheriff and city sets its own cooperation policy—choosing whether to honor ICE detainers, share booking data, or sign 287(g) agreements.

Federal

  • ICE/ERO runs arrests and deportations; CBP manages airports.
  • Immigration courts in Las Vegas decide cases—no state input.
  • DACA/TPS administration, detention standards, and removal priorities.

State

  • Publishes model policies (AG) and workforce plans (Lt. Gov.).
  • Controls Guard deployments and state funds that support ICE partnerships.
  • Can expand or restrict access to licenses, tuition, and benefits.

Local

  • Decides to enter 287(g) and whether to honor detainers.
  • Sets jail booking notification rules and data-sharing policies.
  • Can decline participation on constitutional or liability grounds.
287(g) expansion

Where Local Police Are Deputized

Four agencies currently partner with ICE under 287(g) (Warrant Service Officer model):

Las Vegas Metro

Rejoined June 2025 after being labeled “sanctuary.” Jail notifications for specified crimes; no community enforcement. Detainer requests jumped from 32 (2024) to 957 (through Aug 2025).

Douglas County

Signed February 2025. Jail-based cooperation; initial listing error as task force model corrected.

Mineral County

Signed February 2025. Jail-based agreement; task force application pending.

Nye County

Renewed 2020; status under review. Historically supportive of broader ICE cooperation.

Litigation watch: Morais-Hechavarria v. LVMPD argues Dillon’s Rule bars local 287(g) without explicit legislative authorization—could invalidate all Nevada agreements.
Enforcement trend

Detentions and Arrests Are Surging

Arrests

ICE arrests in Nevada rose 300% January–June 2025 vs. 2024; weekly Nevada arrests now ~48 (4× Biden-era pace).

Detention beds

Nevada Southern Detention Center held 461 detainees in Nov 2025 (capacity 250). Henderson at 93, Washoe at 46. ICE exploring a 450-bed tent facility.

Profile of arrests

Roughly 12–14% have no criminal record; ~70% of the rest are nonviolent (traffic and drug charges most common).

Economic stakes

Immigration Is Structural to Nevada’s Economy

Immigrants make up 24% of the workforce (400K workers) and drive core industries: 44.6% of hotel staff and 74% of housekeepers are foreign-born; construction is 36% immigrant labor. Immigrant-led households hold $16.6B in Las Vegas spending power and pay $1.4B in state/local taxes ($507M from undocumented workers).

Labor shortages

79% of construction firms report hiring difficulty; Las Vegas leaders cite a 100K worker shortfall.

Business ownership

43,700 immigrant business owners—30% of all self-employed Nevadans.

Top sectors

Hospitality (31% immigrant), landscaping (76%), building services (61%), construction (36%).

Legal flashpoints

Cases That Could Rewrite the Rules

Morais-Hechavarria v. LVMPD

Dillon’s Rule challenge to Metro’s 287(g) agreement; outcome could void local authority to partner with ICE statewide.

Garay v. City of Las Vegas & ICE

Detainer practices challenged as Fourth Amendment violations; court denied motion to dismiss.

ACLU v. ICE (Class Action)

Challenges mandatory detention without bond hearings; could alter detention standards in Nevada facilities.

What Voters Should Watch

Policy Choices on the 2026 Ballot

1) How far should Nevada cooperate with ICE?

Future governors control Guard deployments, FEMA-funded infrastructure approvals, and whether to encourage or discourage local 287(g) agreements.

2) Should the Legislature clarify local authority?

A state law could either authorize or prohibit local participation, ending the current patchwork. Dillon’s Rule litigation may force the issue.

3) Economic risk vs. enforcement drive

With 9% of workers undocumented, aggressive detention spikes risk labor shortages in hospitality and construction—central to Nevada’s economy.
